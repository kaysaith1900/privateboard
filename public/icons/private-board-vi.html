PrivateBoard · Visual Identity System · v1.0

A round table, five seats carved into the rim

The mark is a single annular form — a thick gold ring with five seats bitten out of its inner edge. The seats are negative space, not added shapes. Their sizes encode a hierarchy: the top seat is the smallest (the question), the lower seats are the largest (the human side of the table). The system that extends from this — palette, type, lockups, icon family — keeps the editorial register: warm slate paper, champagne gold, restraint over ornament.

01 · the mark

Construction & anatomy

The mark is built on a 100-unit grid. An outer circle at r=46, an inner cutout at r=30 — together they describe a ring of 16-unit thickness. Five seat cutouts sit on the inner edge at regular pentagon positions (72° apart, top-anchored), with deliberately unequal radii: top is 4, upper sides 9, lower sides 13. The lower cuts nearly slice through the ring — only a 3-unit outer band remains. The bottom of the ring (180°) carries no cutout — the solid front face is the side the viewer sits on.

Five seats, unequally weighted

  • — grid 100 × 100 units. Centre at (50, 50). All seat positions resolve to regular pentagon angles (72° apart) at the inner edge (r=30 from centre).
  • — ring Outer at r=46, inner cutout at r=30. The ring is 16 units thick. Hollow centre stays dark; the ring carries the mark.
  • — top seat 0° · r=4 · smallest. A narrow notch into the inner rim — the question being asked, the chair speaking.
  • — upper sides 72° / 288° · r=9 · the AI directors. Mid-depth bites — the inner half of the ring opens up.
  • — lower sides 144° / 216° · r=13 · the human side. Largest bites — they slice through 81% of the ring, leaving only a 3-unit outer sliver. Chair (system) + user (you).
  • — front The bottom of the ring (180°) carries no cutout. The solid front face is the side that faces the viewer — a deliberate gap in the pentagon rotation.
  • — colour Single fill across the ring · champagne gold on dark, deep brass on light. The cutouts inherit the background — they are not drawn.
02 · size scale

Five sizes, one simplification

From 240-pixel hero to 16-pixel favicon. The full mark (5 seats, asymmetric radii) holds down to 40 px. At 22 px and below, the four upper seats collapse into a simplified 3-cut form — one open top notch + two deep lower bites. The hierarchy is preserved in spirit (top is small, sides are large) but the asymmetric pentagon resolves cleanly at favicon scale.

03 · clear space

The room the mark asks for

Always leave at least one ring-thickness of empty space on every side. That's 16 grid units — the same width as the ring itself, mirroring the form's own proportions. No type, image edge, or sibling mark crosses into the dashed frame.

One ring-thickness, every side

The clearance unit is the ring's own thickness — 16 grid units at default scale. Symmetric on all four sides. Above and below the mark are the highest-violation edges (tagline + sibling lockups crowd those zones first).

Print and digital both honour this. When the mark is shrunk to favicon scale, the dashed frame scales with it — half a pixel of crowding at 16 px is the same visual violation as 16 px of crowding at hero scale.

Never let any element enter the dashed frame
04 · colour

Warm near-black, one accent

The palette is monochromatic. The primary register is warm near-black slate with a single accent — champagne gold. A deep brass variant carries the mark on cream paper for print. There is no secondary chromatic accent. Greys are warm-toned (yellow-brown undertone, R ≥ G > B), never cool blues.

05 · wordmark & lockups

private board, three arrangements

The wordmark is a single serif word: private board · lowercase · Charter or its system equivalent · weight 400 · slight optical tightening at display sizes only. The tagline rides beneath in mono uppercase with a slash separator: ROUND TABLE / FIVE SEATS / PRIVATE CONSENSUS. Lowercase preserves the editorial register — no marketing-deck Title Case anywhere.

09 · misuse

Six things not to do

The mark is a small piece of geometry whose meaning lives in the cutouts — what's not drawn. Most violations come from adding things: fills, hard shadows, rotation, equalising seats, decorating the cuts. The remedy is always to remove.

— don't
Fill the hollow centre. The empty middle is the room — fill it and the mark reads as a coin.
— don't
Make all five seats the same size. The hierarchy is the brand — without it, the mark loses its argument.
— don't
Rotate. The top seat sits at the top, by axiom — tilted, the hierarchy reads as broken.
— don't
Gradient the gold. The system is flat — one tone per surface, no metallic sheens, no transitions.
— don't
Put coloured dots inside the cuts. The cuts are negative space — populating them turns the mark into a status indicator.
— do
Leave it alone. The mark works because it does less.
